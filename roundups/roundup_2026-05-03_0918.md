# Weekly Round-Up — Week 18, 2026
*Based on 7 daily briefings · 27 April to 3 May 2026*

## Summary of the Week

[ASML's planned expansion of EUV lithography production](https://the-decoder.com/the-company-with-a-monopoly-on-ais-most-critical-machine-is-racing-to-build-more/) represents a critical supply-side constraint on global AI chip manufacturing, positioning the Netherlands as a strategic chokepoint in the global AI value chain—a role that creates both competitive advantage and geopolitical vulnerability. European AI startups demonstrated significant capital-raising momentum, with [Ineffable Intelligence securing a record €1.1 billion seed round](https://tech.eu/2026/04/27/ineffable-intelligence-launches-with-record-breaking-11b-seed-round/) and [Nebius acquiring US-based Eigen AI for $643 million](https://tech.eu/2026/05/01/nebius-announces-construction-of-one-of-europes-largest-data-centres/), signalling both investor confidence in European AI and the strategic necessity for European firms to acquire US capabilities. However, these positive signals were tempered by mounting concerns about AI business model sustainability—with [OpenAI reportedly missing revenue targets](https://the-decoder.com/openai-misses-revenue-targets-as-anthropic-and-google-close-in/) and major tech companies facing criticism for massive capital misallocation in AI infrastructure. Geopolitical tensions intensified as [China blocked Meta's $2 billion acquisition of AI startup Manus](https://the-decoder.com/china-blocks-metas-2-billion-acquisition-of-ai-startup-manus/), underscoring how AI industrial policy is becoming a central battleground in US-China competition, with implications for European strategic autonomy. Finally, expert discourse highlighted growing concerns about AI safety, premature deployment without adequate guardrails, and the erosion of safety commitments in government partnerships—issues that will likely shape European AI regulation and startup operating conditions.

## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

**AI Safety and Premature Deployment Risks** dominated expert discourse throughout the week. [Gary Marcus raised urgent concerns about AI agents being rolled out too quickly without adequate safety guardrails](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2048757233174122827), citing real-world "vibe-coded disasters" where users lost critical data despite relying on system prompts and safeguards, and [arguing that untrained developers are deploying probabilistic AI systems for critical tasks](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2048742983684784239). This concern persisted mid-week, with [Marcus documenting persistent trust failures in generative AI systems, citing an incident where Claude Opus 4.7 mass-emailed an entire database 20 times per contact despite explicit safety rules](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049565638402957721). By week's end, [Marcus argued that generative AI has become a net negative for society outside narrow use cases like coding](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2050750631342907725), citing harms including educational undermining, mass surveillance, disinformation, and cybercrime. In contrast, [Yann LeCun countered that AI has had one of the safest technology rollouts in history, with problems affecting only a tiny fraction of billions of users](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2048821958087717011).

**European Digital Sovereignty and Strategic Positioning** emerged as a recurring theme. [Thierry Breton argued that Europe is not "behind" in the digital world but rather lacks courage to leverage its existing assets—including infrastructure, industrial champions, a 450-million-person market, and regulatory influence—to shape global standards](https://x.com/ThierryBreton/status/2048695766043033654), framing [digital sovereignty as non-optional in geopolitical competition](https://x.com/ThierryBreton/status/2048748200777990246). By week's end, [Breton criticized the Turnberry agreements between the US and EU, arguing that unilateral US tariff increases demonstrate the EU accepted "submission and humiliation"](https://x.com/ThierryBreton/status/2050539769600282745), raising questions about European technological autonomy in AI-driven competition.

**AI Business Model Sustainability and Capital Allocation** became increasingly prominent as the week progressed. [Gary Marcus argued that OpenAI is in financial trouble after missing revenue and user growth targets](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2048983482685440365), while [major tech companies (Amazon, Google, Microsoft, Meta) are experiencing massive capital misallocation in AI, spending more monthly than the entire Manhattan Project annually while generating minimal profits](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049612941419565456). [Marcus highlighted Oracle's financial vulnerability—with over $300 billion of its $553 billion in performance obligations tied to OpenAI](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049668133397443069), and cited [Mark Cuban's assessment that OpenAI is "shitting away money at scale"](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049674809710428561).

**AI Safety, Government Partnerships, and Surveillance Concerns** surfaced mid-week. [Marcus flagged Google's Pentagon deal as potentially enabling autonomous weapons and mass surveillance](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049104294415409347), noting that [Google has committed to modifying safety filters at government request without legal restrictions—contrasting unfavorably with OpenAI's approach](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049218972923592712). Later, [Sam Altman announced OpenAI's rollout of GPT-5.5-Cyber, a frontier cybersecurity model, to critical infrastructure defenders](https://x.com/sama/status/2049712078836170843), signalling emerging public-private governance models for sensitive AI capabilities.

**Developer Infrastructure and AI-Driven Code Distribution** emerged as a structural concern. [Thomas Wolf discussed how AI agents may disrupt traditional code repositories like GitHub by enabling direct code discovery and retrieval through chat interfaces](https://x.com/Thom_Wolf/status/2049282089518784640), potentially fragmenting the centralized developer ecosystem.

**Intellectual Property and Competitive Dynamics** became a focal point. [Yann LeCun criticized major labs for "pulling the ladder"—using distillation and web data to build dominant positions, then blocking competitors from similar practices](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2050039348679024779), while [Gary Marcus noted OpenAI's aggressive legal tactics](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2049904811165008099). By week's end, [LeCun defended open-source AI development against what he characterized as efforts by "doomers" to restrict access](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2050177061847720371), arguing that bans on model distillation would concentrate power among proprietary American companies.

**Political Threats to Scientific Institutions** surfaced late in the week. [Yann LeCun highlighted the Trump administration's dismantling of over 100 scientific advisory panels and threats to broadcast licenses for critical media](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2049641252287607241), characterizing these as attacks on science and democratic institutions that undermine the institutional foundations for responsible AI policy development.

## Summary

The European Commission has secured political agreement on the Digital Omnibus on AI, which substantially delays implementation of high-risk AI regulations to late 2027 or 2028 while easing compliance requirements for SMEs—a direct response to concerns that the original AI Act threatened European competitiveness. This regulatory reprieve creates a critical window for Dutch startups and investors to develop AI solutions without immediate compliance burdens, though the labeling requirements for deepfakes and AI-generated content remain on track for August 2026. Concurrently, the Commission is launching targeted funding through the Digital Europe Programme for AI-driven regulatory compliance solutions, signaling that while timelines have extended, the EU remains committed to channeling innovation toward governance infrastructure rather than leaving compliance to market forces alone. The pattern suggests a deliberate recalibration: rather than abandoning AI regulation, Brussels is sequencing implementation to allow startup ecosystem maturation while maintaining guardrails on high-risk applications, including explicit bans on non-consensual intimate imagery tools.

## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

Expert discourse across the week centred on three interconnected themes:

**AI System Reliability and Risk in Critical Applications** — Leading researchers including Gary Marcus, David Timis, and Michael Wooldridge (Oxford) warned that generative AI models are fundamentally unreliable for critical functions due to their probabilistic nature. Experts cautioned that the prevailing "move fast and break things" culture in AI development poses serious risks, with Wooldridge flagging a potential "Hindenburg moment" that could undermine public trust in AI technology. This discussion reflects growing concern among the research community about deployment of AI in high-stakes domains without adequate safeguards.

**European Digital Sovereignty and Strategic Autonomy** — EU Commissioner Thierry Breton emphasized that Europe possesses strong strategic assets for digital sovereignty but must deploy them strategically. This theme recurred across expert networks discussing European technological independence versus US dominance in AI development, reflecting broader policy concerns about European competitiveness and strategic autonomy in the AI era.

**AI's Impact on the Labour Market and Skills** — Experts debated the future role of programmers and knowledge workers in an AI-augmented economy. While some argued coding will diminish, others highlighted that the most valued employees will be those who can extract meaningful signals from AI outputs. David Timis noted that the gap between companies capable of executing AI and those unable to do so is widening, underscoring the importance of AI skills and organizational capability as competitive differentiators.

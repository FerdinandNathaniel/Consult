# Weekly Round-Up — Week 24, 2026
*Based on 7 daily briefings · 8 June to 14 June 2026*

## Summary of the Week

The week was dominated by two major regulatory and competitive developments with direct implications for the Dutch and European AI startup ecosystem. First, [the European Commission published a Code of Practice on AI-generated content marking](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/news/commission-publishes-code-practice-marking-and-labelling-ai-generated-content), operationalizing transparency obligations that become legally binding under the AI Act from 2 August 2026—creating immediate compliance requirements for Dutch AI providers and deployers ahead of an [info session on 22 June](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/events/info-session-code-practice-transparency-ai-generated-content-signature-process). Second, [the US government imposed export controls on Anthropic's advanced models (Fable 5 and Mythos 5)](https://www.politico.com/news/2026/06/13/inside-the-whirlwind-24-hours-that-led-the-white-house-to-slap-export-controls-on-anthropic-00961519), effectively blocking European and international access to frontier AI capabilities and signalling arbitrary regulatory risk that will reshape investor pricing of AI infrastructure bets. These developments underscore a widening divergence between EU regulatory clarity (transparency and liability frameworks) and US regulatory unpredictability (export controls), while [China's $295 billion AI infrastructure investment with 80% domestic chip sourcing](https://the-decoder.com/beijings-295-billion-ai-buildout-would-require-80-percent-domestic-chips-locking-out-us-suppliers/) intensifies geopolitical competition. European AI startups face tightening access to frontier models, rising compliance costs, and economic headwinds: [German corporate insolvencies rose 15.8% year-over-year in March 2026](https://x.com/Schuldensuehner/status/2065343014919159849), while [Germany's entire DAX is smaller than SpaceX alone](https://x.com/Schuldensuehner/status/2066051484110749898), highlighting structural competitive disadvantages.

## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

**AI Industry Economics and Viability Concerns** dominated social discourse. [Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2064041550527508785) highlighted research indicating AI companies face severe financial pressures, with [Wharton research suggesting AI must achieve 2.7x productivity gains or risk bankruptcy](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2064041550527508785), while [semiconductor valuations trading at 25x sales versus historical 5x appear stretched](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2064041550527508785). [OpenAI's confidential IPO filing suggested "financials make no sense"](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2064101427866439688), and [hyperscaler capex is approaching telecom bubble levels as a percentage of operating cash flow](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2065172810314780962). These concerns directly challenge the investment thesis underlying current AI infrastructure buildout and have implications for Dutch and European VC allocation decisions.

**AI Capability Claims vs. Reality** emerged as a recurring theme. [A new benchmark (SciConBench) showed frontier AI agents cannot synthesize scientific conclusions well](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2065165484920815711), contradicting industry claims about AI as a scientific tool. [Research demonstrated the vast gap between simplified "neural networks" in AI systems and actual biological neurons](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2065190597472694607), undermining foundational assumptions about current AI architectures. [Gary Marcus](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2064158736688836763) argued that labs deliberately promote narratives of mass unemployment and white-collar job displacement to justify continued funding, despite models not yet delivering such capabilities.

**Open-Source and Local AI Models as Market Alternative** signalled a potential shift in competitive dynamics. [Stanford research showed local models now answer 71.3% of real-world queries accurately (up from 23.2% in 2023) at a fraction of frontier API costs](https://x.com/ylecun/status/2064082422010925178), suggesting a multi-model future where open-source and smaller models dominate most workloads. [Meta, Hugging Face, NVIDIA, and others expanded collaboration on OpenEnv's open agentic RL stack](https://x.com/Thom_Wolf/status/2064021681723547793), indicating institutional momentum behind decentralized AI development—relevant to European startups' ability to compete without reliance on US frontier models.

**European Regulatory and Competitive Positioning** reflected urgent policy concerns. [IMF Managing Director Kristalina Georgieva called for "aggressive regulatory house cleaning,"](https://x.com/DavidTimis/status/2064293987905638470) while [Belgium PM Bart De Wever warned that "China's strategy will eat the EU's initiatives for breakfast" unless Europe coordinates better](https://x.com/DavidTimis/status/2064304804021592158). These statements underscore the policy consensus that European fragmentation undermines competitiveness against both US and Chinese AI strategies.

**US Government Intervention and Regulatory Precedent** became the week's dominant policy story. [Gary Marcus warned that without "bipartisan and independent scientific oversight," export controls give government excessive discretionary power while creating business uncertainty](https://x.com/GaryMarcus/status/2065860248599253222). [David Timis noted this sets a precedent that will force investors to price in regulatory risk, potentially undermining frontier AI labs' ability to fund large compute commitments](https://x.com/DavidTimis/status/2065842839234199837). The concern is that this action signals AI companies can weaponize government against competitors rather than compete on merit—a dynamic with spillover implications for European startups' strategic positioning.

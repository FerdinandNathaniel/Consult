## What Were Followed Accounts Discussing This Week?

Expert discourse on AI policy and startup ecosystems centred on three interconnected themes:

**AI System Reliability and Safety in Critical Applications**

Leading AI researchers and policy experts warned that generative AI models remain fundamentally unreliable for critical tasks due to their probabilistic nature. Gary Marcus, David Timis, and Michael Wooldridge (Oxford) cautioned that the prevailing "move fast and break things" culture poses serious risks, with Wooldridge warning of a potential "Hindenburg moment" that could undermine public trust in AI technology. This discussion reflects growing concern among European policymakers about AI governance frameworks and liability regimes, directly relevant to the EU AI Act implementation and Dutch regulatory strategy.

**European Digital Sovereignty and Strategic Autonomy**

Thierry Breton emphasized that Europe possesses strong strategic assets for digital sovereignty but must deploy them strategically to avoid technological dependence on US or Chinese platforms. This theme aligns with broader EU policy discussions on technological independence, cross-border data governance, and the role of European startups in building sovereign AI capabilities—critical context for Dutch startup positioning within the EU's strategic autonomy agenda.

**Labour Market Transformation and Skills Policy**

Experts debated the future role of programmers and knowledge workers in an AI-augmented economy. While some argued that coding will diminish, others highlighted that the most valued employees will be those who can extract meaningful signals from AI outputs and synthesize complex information. David Timis noted that the gap between companies capable of deploying AI and those unable to do so is widening, raising questions about skills inequality and the need for proactive workforce development strategies in the Netherlands and across Europe.

## Most Relevant Items

### EU AI Act Implementation and High-Risk Classification Guidance
**Mon 19 May, Tue 20 May**

The European Commission released [draft guidelines on the classification of high-risk AI systems](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/library/draft-commission-guidelines-classification-high-risk-ai-systems) under Article 6 of the AI Act, with a [targeted consultation period running through 23 June 2026](https://digital-strategy.ec.europa.eu/en/consultations/targeted-consultation-draft-guidelines-classification-high-risk-artificial-intelligence-systems). These guidelines directly address implementation uncertainty by clarifying how providers and deployers should assess whether their systems trigger high-risk designation, a critical threshold determining compliance obligations and market access conditions. For Dutch AI ventures and their investors, these guidelines represent a pivotal regulatory clarification that will either reduce compliance friction or, if overly restrictive, create new barriers to market entry and capital deployment.

### Dutch Government Regulatory Sovereignty Under Pressure: Tech Companies Disclose Names of Dutch Officials to US Senate
**Fri 23 May**

[Tech companies including Microsoft and Meta disclosed names of Dutch civil servants and scientists to a US Senate committee investigating alleged "tech censorship,"](https://nos.nl/l/2615391) prompting the Dutch government to characterize the disclosure as "extremely concerning." The officials targeted are those implementing EU digital governance frameworks. The Dutch government fears that identified officials face potential consequences including entry bans or sanctions from US authorities, citing precedent of previous pressure tactics against EU regulators. This incident reveals a structural tension in the AI policy environment: as the Netherlands and EU advance regulatory frameworks for platform governance, US political actors are actively targeting the officials responsible for enforcement, which could chill regulatory ambition and create asymmetric pressure on Dutch policymakers at a moment when coherent AI governance is critical to the startup and investment climate.
